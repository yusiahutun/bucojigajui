Beloved Pittsburgh TV anchor Jon Burnett, 65, reveals he is suffering from CTE that has stopped him

Publish date: 2024-02-20

A beloved former Pittsburgh TV anchor has revealed he's suffering from CTE which is affecting his ability to walk, talk and eat solid food after he played football in his youth. 

Jon Burnett, 70, was recently diagnosed with the neurological condition - and he says he'll donate his brain to science when he dies to help find a cure. 

The long-running co-host of Evening Magazine and Pittsburgh Today at KDKA-TV played both fullback and defensive end in Knoxville, Tennessee from the age of 10. 

'Ultimately, I paid the price with the concussions that I had over the years, several of them,' Burnett told his co-host of 20 years Kristine Sorensen. He estimated he suffered hundreds of hits to his head over a decade playing football. 

Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) is a degenerative brain disease that is caused by repeated hits to the head, and it's been linked to high-impact sports like football and boxing.

Memory loss is one of the key symptoms, and while speaking with Sorensen, Burnett asked Debbie, his wife of 48 years, to help him remember 'what's wrong with me?' 

'What's wrong with my throat?' he asked hoarsely before adding: 'She's my brains. Somebody would have to be.'

'I spent some of my time wishing that my voice wasn't gone,' Burnett added. 'It's just this is the best I can do. This is a good day.' 

Burnett said his short-term memory has been declining for more than a decade, but his ability to speak and move his facial muscles have markedly deteriorated more recently. 

'It's not as frustrating as it once was, because I'm growing accustomed to it. I guess it's familiar,' he said of the memory loss. 

Burnett said Debbie never leaves his side since he suffered a choking scare and he's since been moved on to a soft-food diet. 

UPMC cognitive neurologist Dr Joseph Malone recently ruled out all other causes of Burnett's decline, diagnosing him with suspected CTE. Doctors can't give a certain diagnosis because the only way to do so is via brain autopsy after death. 

Burnett is taking part in a University of Pittsburgh study into CTE by donating his brain after death and taking part in online questionnaires. 

The university's National Sports Brain Bank is collecting brains from athletes who played sports which involve high risk of sustaining head injuries so they can learn more about why some people get the disease and what can be done to prevent it.

It comes after Boston University researchers announced earlier this month that they have discovered CTE in 345 of 376 former NFL players they've studied.

Researchers at the CTE Center, who have been sounding the alarm about the disease for years, said the 91.7-percent occurrence rate starkly contrasts with the general population's, which is characterized as 'extremely low.'

For perspective, in 2018, the Framingham Heart Study found only case of CTE in 164 brain samples, and that sample came from a former college football player.

Former players diagnosed with CTE in the last year include Philadelphia Eagles quarterback Rick Arrington, Kansas City Chiefs defensive tackle Ed Lothamer, and Denver Broncos receiver Demaryius Thomas, whose family said he showed signs of erratic behavior and paranoia in the years leading up to his death in 2022.

The NFL has continued to adjust its concussion protocols this season after receiving criticism for the handling of several head injuries to Miami Dolphins quarterback Tua Tagovailoa.

McKee likened Adams's brain disease to that of Aaron Hernandez, the former New England Patriots star who was convicted of murder before killing himself in prison. Hernandez was found to have stage 3 CTE.

Adams was 32 when he gunned down Dr. Robert Lesslie, 70, his wife, Barbara, 69, two of their grandchildren - Adah, 9, and Noah, 5 - as well as HVAC technicians James Lewis and Robert Shook, both 38, on April 7, 2021, in Rock Hill, South Carolina.

Police later found Adams with a self-inflicted gunshot wound to the head. Adams's family agreed to send his brain to Boston University to be tested.

Adams, who suffered a pair of concussions in a three-week span in 2012, had applied for disability benefits from the NFL prior to his death, but was struggling to get approval, according to his family.

Similarly, former NFL wide receiver Vincent Jackson was found to have Stage 2 CTE after being discovered dead in a hotel room in 2021.

His family suspected CTE because he sustained multiple concussions during a 12-year NFL career. However, he was never officially diagnosed with a traumatic head injury, according to team records.

That's not uncommon. About 20 percent of people suffering from the brain disease have never been diagnosed with a concussion, according to doctors from the CTE Center at Boston University.
