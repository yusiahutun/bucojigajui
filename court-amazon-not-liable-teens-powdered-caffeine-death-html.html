Amazon is NOT liable for death of Ohio teen who overdosed on powdered caffeine

Publish date: 2024-04-10

Logan Stiner, 18, died of a caffeine overdose at his home in LaGrange, Ohio, in May 2014

Amazon is not liable for the death of a high school senior who overdosed on powdered caffeine purchased through the retail giant because it came from a third-party seller, the Ohio Supreme Court has ruled.  

At issue before the court was whether the state's product liability law, which predates online commerce, could be applied to the facts of the death of Logan Stiner. 

The 18-year-old was just days from high school graduation when his brother found him unresponsive at their family's home in LaGrange, southwest of Cleveland, in May 2014.

Stiner died of cardiac arrhythmia and seizure from acute caffeine toxicity, a coroner ruled. 

He had more than 70 micrograms of caffeine per milliliter of blood in his system - as much as 23 times the amount found in the system of a typical coffee or soda drinker.

What was undisputed is that Stiner - an accomplished wrestler who was voted prom king that year - ingested 'Rhino Hard'-brand powdered caffeine given to him by a friend who bought it on Amazon and was using it as a 'pre-workout' boost. 

What the court decided Thursday was that Amazon can't be held liable in cases where third-party sellers access Amazon's 'fulfillment by Amazon' program.

Stiner, who is pictured during a wrestling match, had been using the 'Rhino Hard'-brand powdered caffeine purchased by his friend on Amazon as a pre-workout boost

'Because Amazon does not have a relationship with the manufacturers of third-party products, Amazon lacks control over product safety,' Justice Judi French wrote in the court's unanimous ruling. 

'Amazon did not choose to offer the caffeine powder for sale and has no role in manufacturing, labeling or packaging the product.'

Justice Michael Donnelly sided with the ruling but said the court was limited by Ohio's outdated product liability laws. 

'The divide between the pre-Internet age and the current age is so profound that laws like this Act might as well have been written in the stone age,' he wrote.

Donnelly suggested that an updating of such laws would ensure 'the utmost protection' for Ohio consumers.

The caffeine product Stiner overdosed on (pictured) came from a third-party seller using Amazon's fulfillment program

Messages were left for Amazon and an attorney representing them before the state Supreme Court. 

Brian Balser, an attorney representing Stiner's father, called the decision disappointing but took heart from Donnelly's arguments.

The goal was never to reach a settlement with Amazon but to raise awareness, and the court has created an opportunity to do that, Balser said Thursday.

'They opened the door for us to go to the Ohio Legislature, and say: "Listen, we've got to update this,"' Balser said. 'They clearly put the protection of Ohio consumers on the state Ohio legislature.'

In his arguments before the court, Balser had said the company was not a 'neutral platform' in the powder's sale but promoted it, introduced it to Stiner's friend as a customer and played an 'indispensable role' in its sale.

Lawyers for Amazon said the company didn't meet the definition of a supplier under Ohio law - ownership, control and hands-on actions with a product. 

They noted that Stiner's friend has testified she chose to click on the product she then bought.

Stiner's family (above together) argued Amazon was responsible for his death because it promoted the powdered caffeine and sold it to the teen's friend

Stiner had been prom king at his high school and was set to graduate three days before he died

In 2015, Ohio's governor at the time, Republican John Kasich, signed into law a bill inspired by Stiner's death that banned the sale of pure powdered caffeine in the state.

Two lower courts ruled against Stiner's family, and both the Ohio and US chambers of commerce had urged the Ohio Supreme Court to uphold those decisions.

The 3rd US Circuit Court of Appeals in Philadelphia is reviewing a lawsuit against Amazon brought by a Pennsylvania woman who was blinded in one eye after a retractable dog leash she bought snapped and hit her.

In that case, a three-judge panel ruled last year that Amazon could be sued over a defective product sold by one of its third-party vendors. That ruling was then vacated when the full court agreed to hear the case.

The Food and Drug Administration has warned consumers to avoid pure powdered caffeine. Even a teaspoon could be lethal. It is equivalent to about 25 cups of regular coffee, the FDA says.

In 2018, the FDA said supplements consisting of pure or highly concentrated caffeine in powder or liquid forms, often sold in bulk, are 'generally unlawful' when sold directly to consumers.
